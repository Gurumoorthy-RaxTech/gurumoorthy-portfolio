🗣️ In plain English"Basics" at this level doesn't mean easy questions — it means foundational questions asked with zero tolerance for a shaky or rambling answer. They're checking depth of understanding on things you're supposed to already know cold after 3+ years, not testing you on something obscure.
B What "basics" actually means here
The 6 buckets that make up "basics" for an experienced .NET candidate
Infosys panel members-ku romba variety irukkalaam (sometimes non-.NET background-um irukkalaam), so anga safe-a irukura questions ella company-layum common-a irukura fundamentals thaan — niche framework-specific trivia illa.
🗣️ The 6 buckets, in the order they usually get asked
  1. OOP & SOLID — 4 pillars, interface vs abstract class, at least 2-3 SOLID principles with a real example. → Part 2
  2. Core C# — value vs reference type, exception handling, string vs StringBuilder, async/await basics. → Part 1
  3. SQL basics — joins (inner/left especially), group by, basic query writing on the spot, indexing in one sentence. → Part 5, Part 15
  4. ASP.NET Core / Web API basics — middleware pipeline, DI lifetimes, REST principles, status codes. → Part 3, Part 4
  5. Your own project, cold — what it does, your specific role, one metric you improved, one hard problem you solved. → Part 9
  6. Behavioral basics — one conflict story, one mistake-you-owned story, why you're moving, using STAR format. → Part 9
🔁 What's usually NOT asked at this level
Deep infra/DevOps internals (unless the role is explicitly DevOps) — Docker/K8s basics enough, don't need to be an expert.
Obscure design patterns beyond Repository/Factory/Singleton — knowing the common 4-5 well beats knowing 20 shallowly.
Competitive-programming-level DSA — simple array/string/SQL problems, not LeetCode-hard.

E HR-round questions (CTC, notice period, etc.)
The questions that aren't technical but decide the offer
Idhu technical prep illa, aana miss pannina interview crack pannitum offer delay/lose aagalaam. Straight-forward-a, confident-a, honest-a answer pannunga — over-explain pannாma.
🗣️ Typical HR questions + how to frame the answer
  • Current CTC / Expected CTC: Give a clear number or tight range, don't dodge. Base it on your current CTC + a reasonable hike (commonly 20-40% for a lateral move, adjust to your situation).
  • Notice period: State your actual notice period honestly, and mention if it's negotiable/if you have an early-release option.
  • Why are you leaving your current company? Frame it positively — growth, scope, technology exposure — never bad-mouth RAX Tech or your current manager.
  • Why Infosys? Scale of projects, structured career growth, exposure to larger/more diverse client engagements than a smaller company can offer.
  • Relocation / work location: Be clear and decisive — "yes, I'm open" or "I need X" — hesitation here reads as low seriousness about the offer.
F Mistakes that sink experienced candidates
The 6 most common ways a strong resume still fails the interview
  1. Rambling instead of answering. The long essay-style answers are fine for solo revision, but in the actual interview, lead with a 2-3 sentence direct answer, THEN give the example if they want more. Panel members interrupt or mentally check out on long wind-ups.
  2. Can't explain your own resume line. If it's on your resume, you must be able to explain it in one breath. Cut anything you can't confidently defend.
  3. Weak on the fundamentals under your "expert" topics. Someone who's built 5 microservices but can't cleanly state what Scoped vs Singleton means looks worse than someone with less experience who nails the basics.
  4. No concrete numbers/examples. "I improved performance" is weak. "I cut query time by 40% by adding a covering index" is strong — always have the real Zen Campus numbers ready (40%, 35%, 1,000+ users, sub-3-second SMS).
  5. Badmouthing current employer/manager. Always frame the move as growth-seeking, never as escaping something bad.
  6. Not asking any questions back. Skipping "do you have questions for us?" reads as low genuine interest.
G Day-of checklist + questions to ask them
Before you join the call
  • Resume open in one tab, this roadmap's Cheat Sheets open in another.
  • Quiet room, good lighting if video, stable internet tested beforehand.
  • Notepad + pen ready to sketch a quick diagram (middleware pipeline, table joins) if asked.
  • Have your 30-second pitch and 2 behavioral stories rehearsed out loud at least once that day — not just read silently.
Good questions to ask the interviewer at the end
  • "What does the team/project I'd be joining actually work on day to day?"
  • "What does success look like in this role in the first 6 months?"
  • "What's the tech stack and architecture on the project I'd likely be staffed on?"
  • Avoid leading with salary/leave-policy questions here — save those for HR/recruiter conversations.
